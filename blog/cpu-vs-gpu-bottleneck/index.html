CPU vs GPU Bottleneck — Which Is Limiting Your FPS?

Published 2026-07-02

CPU vs GPUbottleneckFPS

Every gaming PC has a limiting component in any given scenario. The two most common limiters are the CPU and the GPU. Knowing which one is holding you back saves money and prevents wasted upgrades.

CPU bottleneck vs GPU bottleneck

CPU bottleneck GPU bottleneck
What limits FPS Processor frame preparation Graphics card rendering
Common at 1080p, esports, simulation games 1440p, 4K, ray tracing
GPU usage Often below 85% Usually 95–100%
Fix Faster CPU, better RAM, lower resolution Stronger GPU, upscaling, lower graphics
Is it bad? Yes, if you want higher FPS at low res Often normal and desirable at high res

How resolution changes the limiter

Resolution is the single biggest factor in whether your build is CPU-bound or GPU-bound:

  • 1080p: The GPU finishes frames quickly. Even mid-range cards can become CPU-limited with a weaker processor.
  • 1440p: More balanced. A good CPU + mid-high GPU pairing often shows a modest GPU bottleneck.
  • 4K: Almost always GPU-bound. The graphics card works at full capacity; CPU choice matters less for average FPS.

This is why a Ryzen 5 5600 paired with an RTX 5090 shows a massive CPU bottleneck at 1080p but may look more balanced at 4K — the GPU finally has enough work to do.

How to identify your limiter

Quick in-game test

  1. Open a demanding game and note your FPS at High settings.
  2. Drop all graphics settings to Low.
  3. If FPS barely changes → likely CPU bottleneck.
  4. If FPS jumps significantly → likely GPU bottleneck.

Which should you upgrade first?

Upgrade GPU first if:

  • You game at 1440p or 4K
  • GPU usage is consistently at 99%
  • You want better visuals, ray tracing, or higher settings

Upgrade CPU first if:

  • You game at 1080p high refresh (144 Hz+)
  • You play esports, simulators, or MMOs
  • You stream or record while gaming
  • GPU usage stays low despite low graphics settings

RAM and storage matter too

While RAM and storage rarely cap average FPS, they cause stutter and long load times. Single-channel RAM, 8 GB capacity, or running games from a hard drive can make a balanced build feel worse than the FPS number suggests.
